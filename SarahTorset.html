Sarah Torset

Sarah Torset

Redmond High School - Grade 11

My project is about the super-void (as compared to a local void) that our galaxy exists in. The average void in the universe is around 90 million to 450 million light years in radius, while the KBC void, the one we’re in, is around 100 billion light years in radius. I also talk about how the voids and unvoided spaces are created by a possible web-like pattern, with voids and areas surrounding the voids that have a much higher relative density level than the voids. With these ideas in mind, I talk about how they help explain the expansion of our universe. The general downside to being a void that is seven times bigger than the average void is that we are so far away from other parts of the universe, and so far away from being able to understand or even see most of what's out there. Even though we can’t see what makes up most of the universe, astronomers can guess that it’s probably a lot of dark matter, because our known universe is 95% dark matter. The general upside to the web shape that our universe supposedly has is that it can explain why our universe is expanding much faster than we originally believed it to be. If the unvoided spaces of our universe are much more dense than the voided spaces of our universe, their gravity will pull the voids outward, offering an explanation to our universal expansion.
